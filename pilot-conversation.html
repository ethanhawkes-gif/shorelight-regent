The thesis

Luxury is memory.

The difference between a vendor and a host is whether they remember you.

In ultra-luxury, the highest compliment is a guest who knows your name. The opposite — a guest who must re-introduce themselves each voyage — is a silent leak of the very equity the brand is built on. Most of what a great host would need to know, the guest has already told the world. The gap is connecting it to the moment of service.

What it is — and isn't

Shorelight informs the crew. It never directs, and never touches the guest automatically.

What it is

Guest intelligence for the people who deliver the experience. A defensible, sourced read — what matters to this guest, ranked, with provenance — that a butler or GM can act on with judgment.

What it isn't

Not surveillance. Not a script. Not an AI talking to your guest. No private data we can't source. The restraint is the product: what we decline to use is shown on purpose.

Think of it as agent-assist for luxury hospitality — augmenting the human touch, never replacing it.

The four questions we should answer together

The honest frame.

  • Data → inference → recommendation. How does a public signal become a service moment — and where does the crew's judgment stay in charge?
  • Sources used, and not used. What's fair game, what's off-limits, and how we make the restraint visible.
  • Guest equity. Capturing the upside — which guests deserve bespoke moments — and protecting the downside — which relationships are at risk and what to do.
  • The pilot. What we'd prove, and how we'd know — honestly — whether it worked.
The pilot we'd want

One ship. One KPI. One guardrail. Treatment vs. control.

  • One primary metric we're trying to move — recognition, advocacy, or repeat intent — declared before we start.
  • One guardrail metric we refuse to harm: the felt experience. The "never surveilled" feeling is measured, not assumed.
  • A contained scope — a single sailing, a clean control group, before-and-after we can defend.
  • Pre-registered success — and a kill rule. A clean null result is a valid, valuable finding.

This is where the marketing scientist and the AI pilot leader agree: know the metric, protect the other lever, crawl before you run.

How we avoid the traps

The fast way fails. We're proposing the right way.

  • Don't be Klarna. Never optimize efficiency at the experience's expense. For Regent: never let "intelligence" tip into surveillance or scripted service.
  • Don't automate the guest. Crew-facing and advisory only — the lower-risk lens, by design.
  • Don't sell the big bang. A staged, measured pilot beats a fleet-wide promise we can't yet stand behind.
  • Solve the right problem. Surface genuine human moments — don't paper over things a better basic would fix.

If there's a there there

Possible next steps.

  • Scope a single-sailing pilot — define the KPI, the guardrail, and the control, together.
  • Confirm the data boundary — exactly which sources are in, which are out, and how restraint is shown to the guest.
  • A short working session with the crew who'd actually use it — their judgment is the final word.
  • Agree the kill rule up front, so a null result is a win for everyone's credibility.

No commitment today. Just: is this worth a careful first step?
